Health & Liability Insurance

Insurance in Germany is not optional—it is a legal requirement. But navigating the complex system of public, private, and liability insurance can be overwhelming.

1. Public Health Insurance (Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung)

For almost all international students under 30, public health insurance (like TK, AOK, Barmer) is the best choice. It costs around €120 - €130 per month.

  • Why choose it? It covers virtually everything. Doctor visits, hospital stays, and essential dental work are paid for. You just show your insurance card and never see a bill.
  • Requirement: It is required to enroll in any public German university.
  • The "Age 30" Rule: Once you turn 30, the student rate expires, and public insurance becomes significantly more expensive (around €200+).

2. Private Health Insurance

Private student insurances (like Mawista or Care Concept) are very cheap (often €30-€50/month), but they come with massive risks.

The Private Insurance Trap

  • With private insurance, you must pay the doctor's bill upfront yourself (which can be hundreds of euros) and then ask the insurance company for a refund.
  • They often reject claims for "pre-existing conditions."
  • Once you opt out of public insurance to take private insurance as a student, you cannot go back to public insurance for the rest of your studies.

Recommendation: Stick to public insurance (TK/AOK) unless you are over 30 or doing a language course where public isn't allowed.

3. Finding a "Hausarzt" (General Practitioner)

In Germany, you do not go directly to a hospital unless it is a life-threatening emergency. Instead, you must register with a Hausarzt (Family Doctor / GP).

  • Register Early: Do not wait until you are sick to find a Hausarzt. Many doctors are full and do not accept new patients ("Aufnahmestopp"). Find one near you using the Doctolib app and register while you are healthy.
  • The "Krankschreibung": If you are too sick to attend a mandatory university exam or your part-time job, you must get a sick note (Krankschreibung/AU-Bescheinigung) from your Hausarzt on the very first day. Without it, you will fail the exam or lose your pay.

Pink vs. Green Prescriptions

When you go to the pharmacy (Apotheke), the color of the paper your doctor gave you is extremely important if you have Public Insurance:

  • The Pink Prescription (Kassenrezept): This means the medicine is medically necessary. Your public insurance pays for almost all of it. You only pay a small co-pay (usually €5).
  • The Green Prescription (Grünes Rezept): This is a recommendation (e.g., for mild cough syrup or vitamins). Your insurance does not cover this. You must pay the full price out of your own pocket.

4. Haftpflichtversicherung (Personal Liability Insurance)

In India, this type of insurance is rare. In Germany, it is considered the most important insurance you can buy after health insurance.

What does it do? If you accidentally break someone's expensive laptop, scratch a parked car with your bicycle, or lose the master key to your apartment building (which can cost thousands of euros to replace all locks), this insurance pays for it.

Cost: It is incredibly cheap—usually around €40 to €60 per year.

You must get this as soon as you land. Providers like Getsafe, Feather, or Check24 offer English support.
