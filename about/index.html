History

Maharashtra through the ages

A civilisation spanning thousands of years. Click any era to expand the full story.

230 BCE
Satavahana Empire: Maharashtra's first great kingdom
▶

The Satavahana dynasty (230 BCE – 220 CE) established one of the earliest and most powerful kingdoms in the Deccan. Based in Pratishthana (modern Paithan), they unified much of central India and were great patrons of art and trade.

  • Controlled major trade routes between northern and southern India
  • Built the earliest Buddhist cave complexes at Ajanta and Karla
  • Their currency and administration influenced the entire Deccan region
  • Prakrit language flourished under their patronage
600–1200 CE
Chalukyas, Rashtrakutas & the golden age of cave art
▶

Maharashtra witnessed successive waves of powerful dynasties who left an indelible mark on art, architecture, and literature. The Badami Chalukyas and Rashtrakutas built some of the most spectacular rock-cut temples in the world.

  • Ellora Caves (UNESCO) - 34 monasteries and temples carved into rock between 600–1000 CE
  • Ajanta Caves (UNESCO) - Buddhist murals considered the finest surviving examples of ancient Indian art
  • Rashtrakuta king Dantidurga established dominion over much of India
  • Kailasa Temple at Ellora - carved top-down from a single rock, an engineering marvel
1630–1680
Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj: birth of the Maratha Empire
▶

Shivaji Maharaj is the defining figure of Maharashtrian identity. Born in 1630 at Shivneri fort, he founded the Maratha Empire and built a navy, a civil administration, and a military force that challenged the Mughal Empire at its peak.

  • Captured his first fort - Torna - at just 16 years of age
  • Created India's first modern navy to protect the Konkan coast
  • Coronation at Raigad Fort in 1674 - established Hindavi Swarajya
  • Known for his treatment of women with respect and protection of all faiths
  • Guerrilla warfare tactics in the Sahyadri mountains remain studied in military history
1680–1818
The Peshwa era: Maratha Empire at its greatest extent
▶

After Shivaji, the Peshwas (Prime Ministers) based in Pune expanded the Maratha Empire to cover nearly two-thirds of the Indian subcontinent. At its peak, the Maratha Confederacy was the dominant power in India.

  • Peshwa Bajirao I (1720–1740) - widely regarded as the greatest cavalry general of his time, never lost a battle
  • Maratha influence extended from Attock (Pakistan) to Thanjavur (Tamil Nadu)
  • Third Battle of Panipat (1761) - a turning point that weakened the confederacy
  • Pune became a major centre of art, literature, and governance
1818–1947
British era: reformers, thinkers, and the freedom movement
▶

Under British rule, Maharashtra became a crucible of social reform and freedom struggle. Some of India's most influential thinkers and freedom fighters emerged from Marathi society.

  • Jyotirao Phule (1827–1890) - championed education for women and lower castes, founded the first school for girls
  • Lokmanya Bal Gangadhar Tilak - launched "Swaraj is my birthright" and popularised Ganesh Chaturthi as a public festival
  • Dr. B.R. Ambedkar - from Mhow, Madhya Pradesh but deeply Maharashtrian - drafted India's Constitution
  • Savitribai Phule - India's first female teacher and pioneering social reformer
1 May 1960
Maharashtra State formed: Marathi pride realised
▶

After years of the Samyukta Maharashtra movement, Maharashtra was carved out as a separate state on 1 May 1960 - now celebrated annually as Maharashtra Day (Maharashtra Din). Mumbai became its capital.

  • The movement was led by figures like Keshavrao Jedhe and Senapati Bapat
  • 105 people lost their lives in the movement - honoured at Hutatma Chowk, Mumbai
  • Maharashtra quickly became India's industrial and financial capital
  • Bombay (Mumbai) emerged as Asia's film capital - Bollywood
Present
Modern Maharashtra: global diaspora, enduring culture
▶

Today Maharashtra is India's largest economy and most urbanised state. Marathi people work across every profession in every continent - while keeping their language, food, festivals, and identity alive.

  • Mumbai is India's financial capital and home to the world's largest film industry
  • Pune is one of Asia's fastest-growing tech and education hubs
  • An estimated 3+ million Marathi people live outside India
  • Ganesh Chaturthi is now celebrated in 50+ countries worldwide
  • Maharashtra in Europe is part of this global story - bringing the community together across the continent

About Maharashtra in Europe

Maharashtra in Europe was founded with a single conviction: the Marathi diaspora in Europe deserves a proper digital home. Millions of Marathi people have made Europe their home — in London's finance corridors, Frankfurt's engineering firms, Amsterdam's tech campuses, and Zurich's banks. We curate events, run Sahayak (a travel companion finder for Marathi travellers between Europe and India), and document Maharashtra's culture, history, and diaspora stories.

Maharashtra: Key Facts

Population — 126 Million

Maharashtra is home to over 126 million people — more than most countries in the world. Mumbai's metropolitan population is ~21 million; Pune is one of India's fastest-growing cities at ~7 million. Literacy rate is 82%, above the national average. An estimated 3+ million Maharashtrians live outside India.

Marathi Language — 83 Million Speakers

Marathi is spoken by over 83 million people worldwide, making it the 10th most spoken language on earth. It is the official language of Maharashtra and co-official in Goa and Daman & Diu. Marathi has a recorded literary history of over 900 years. Dnyaneshwari (1290 CE) by Sant Dnyaneshwar is a landmark in world literature. Marathi is written in Devanagari script and has over 40 dialects including Varhadi, Ahirani, Kolhapuri, and Malvani.

36 Administrative Districts

Maharashtra is divided into 36 districts across six divisions: Konkan (the coastal strip including Mumbai, known for beaches and seafood), Nashik (the wine capital of India and Kumbh Mela site), Pune (the cultural capital, home of the Peshwas and IT industry), Aurangabad (gateway to Ajanta and Ellora UNESCO caves), Amravati (Vidarbha region, cotton farming and tiger reserves), and Nagpur (India's geographic centre, famous for oranges).

Statehood: 1 May 1960

Maharashtra was formed on 1 May 1960, celebrated annually as Maharashtra Din. It was the outcome of the Samyukta Maharashtra movement. Before 1960, the region was part of Bombay State. 105 people were martyred in police firing during protests — honoured at Hutatma Chowk, Mumbai. Key leaders: Keshavrao Jedhe, Senapati Bapat, S.M. Joshi, and Acharya Atre. Maharashtra quickly became India's industrial and financial capital; Bombay (Mumbai) emerged as Asia's film capital.

5 UNESCO World Heritage Sites

  • Ajanta Caves (2nd century BCE) — 30 rock-cut Buddhist cave monuments with the world's finest ancient murals
  • Ellora Caves (600–1000 CE) — 34 monasteries and temples of Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain faiths carved into one cliff
  • Elephanta Caves — island near Mumbai, dedicated to Shiva with the iconic Trimurti sculpture
  • Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus — the Gothic Victorian railway station in Mumbai, still fully operational
  • Victorian Gothic and Art Deco Ensembles of Mumbai — the Oval Maidan colonial-era precinct

Economic Powerhouse — 14% of India's GDP

Maharashtra contributes approximately 14% of India's total GDP — more than any other state. Mumbai is home to the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), Asia's oldest stock exchange, and the highest concentration of Fortune 500 offices in India. Pune leads in IT and automobile manufacturing. The state accounts for ~40% of India's FDI inflows. Key industries: finance, IT, pharmaceuticals, automobiles, textiles, Bollywood, and agriculture.

Marathi Community Across Europe

London, United Kingdom — 15,000+ Marathi

London is home to the largest Marathi community in Europe, with an estimated 15,000+ residents. Harrow, Wembley, and Southall have the highest concentrations of Marathi families; Surrey and Hertfordshire are popular among professionals. Marathi Vishwa UK is one of the oldest Marathi organisations in Europe. Ganesh Chaturthi is celebrated publicly in London. The community is heavily represented in finance, NHS healthcare, information technology, and academia.

Frankfurt & Germany — 8,000+ Marathi

Germany hosts one of the fastest-growing Marathi communities in Europe, with 8,000+ speakers across Frankfurt, Munich, Stuttgart, and Berlin. Germany's Blue Card visa scheme actively recruits skilled engineers and IT professionals. Frankfurt is Germany's financial capital; Stuttgart and Munich attract automotive engineers at BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Bosch, and Siemens. Maharashtra Mandal Frankfurt is one of the oldest Marathi organisations in Germany. Annual Ganesh Utsav celebrations are held across German cities.

Amsterdam, Netherlands — 5,000+ Marathi

Amsterdam and the Netherlands are home to 5,000+ Marathi-speaking people drawn by the Netherlands' welcoming immigration policies and strong tech sector. Major employers include ASML, Philips, and ING. Ganesh Chaturthi is celebrated in The Hague and Amsterdam annually. The kennismigrant (highly skilled migrant) visa makes relocation from India accessible.

Paris, France — 4,000+ Marathi

Paris hosts 4,000+ Marathi-speaking people, with communities also in Lyon and Toulouse. Airbus in Toulouse attracts Marathi aerospace engineers. LVMH, L'Oreal, and luxury sector companies employ Marathi professionals. Renault-Nissan draws Marathi automotive engineers. UNESCO headquarters in Paris has Indian professionals including Marathi individuals. Annual Ganesh Chaturthi celebrations draw Marathi families from across France.

Zurich, Switzerland — 3,000+ Marathi

Zurich's Marathi community of ~3,000+ is among the most professionally accomplished in Europe, concentrated in finance, pharmaceuticals, and engineering. UBS and Zurich Insurance employ many Marathi professionals. Roche, Novartis, and other Basel-area pharma giants draw Marathi scientists. ABB and other engineering firms attract Marathi engineers. Switzerland's high quality of life draws Marathi families planning long-term stays.

Vienna, Austria — 2,000+ Marathi

Vienna is unique in the European Marathi landscape — characterised by UN employees, academics, and diplomats. Vienna hosts major UN agencies including IAEA, UNODC, and UNIDO. OPEC headquarters draws energy sector professionals. The University of Vienna and TU Wien attract Indian academics and researchers. Growing interest in Vienna as a base for Marathi professionals within EU institutions.

Maharashtra Festivals

Ganesh Chaturthi

Ganesh Chaturthi is Maharashtra's most iconic festival — a 10-day celebration of Lord Ganesha held on the 4th day of Bhadrapada (August–September). Clay idols of Ganesha are installed in homes and public pandals; on Anant Chaturdashi they are immersed in water (Visarjan). Mumbai's Lalbaugcha Raja attracts millions. The public festival format was popularised by Lokmanya Bal Gangadhar Tilak in 1893 as a community-building and political-awakening tool during British rule. Ganesh Chaturthi is now celebrated in 50+ countries including London, Amsterdam, and Frankfurt.

Gudi Padwa — Marathi New Year

Gudi Padwa marks the Marathi New Year on the first day of Chaitra (March–April). Every Marathi household raises a Gudi — a bamboo stick topped with bright silk cloth, neem leaves, sugar crystals (sakhar), and a copper pot (kalash) — placed at the entrance as a symbol of victory. Traditional dishes include Shrikhand, Puran Poli, and Soonth Panak. New business ventures are traditionally started on Gudi Padwa.

Wari — Pandharpur Pilgrimage

The Pandharpur Wari is Maharashtra's most sacred pilgrimage — hundreds of thousands of Warkari devotees walk to the Vitthal temple in Pandharpur, singing the abhangas of saints Dnyaneshwar and Tukaram. Held twice a year: Ashadhi Ekadashi (June–July) and Kartiki Ekadashi (October–November). At peak, over 1 million people join. In 2026, the Pandharpur Wari went global — devotees carried the padukas from London through 11 countries over 50 days to reach Pandharpur.

Bail Pola — Bull Festival

Bail Pola is a uniquely Maharashtrian agricultural festival honouring the bull, celebrated on the new moon day of Shravana (August–September). Bulls are bathed, decorated with garlands, and paraded with drumbeats. In Vidarbha and Marathwada, it is one of the most important festivals of the year. In cities and among the diaspora, toy clay or wooden bulls are decorated as a nod to the tradition.

Maharashtra Food

Vada Pav

Vada Pav is Maharashtra's most beloved street food — sometimes called "the Indian burger." A deep-fried spiced potato dumpling (batata vada) inside a soft bun (pav), served with dry garlic chutney (sukha lasun chutney) and green chilli chutney. Invented in the 1960s near Dadar station, Mumbai by Ashok Vaidya to feed mill workers. Now available across Indian restaurants in London, Amsterdam, and other European cities.

Puran Poli

Puran Poli is Maharashtra's most celebrated sweet dish — a golden flatbread stuffed with chana dal cooked with jaggery and cardamom. Served warm with ghee, milk, or katachi amti (spiced dal broth). Central to Holi, Diwali, Ganesh Chaturthi, and weddings. Every Marathi household has its own recipe passed down through generations.

Modak

Modak is the sweet dumpling synonymous with Ganesh Chaturthi — considered Lord Ganesha's favourite food. A steamed dumpling (ukdiche modak) made from rice flour, stuffed with fresh coconut and jaggery. 21 modaks are traditionally offered to Ganesha during Chaturthi puja. Modern versions include chocolate, dry fruit, and mawa modak.

Misal Pav

Misal Pav is a beloved Maharashtrian breakfast — fiery sprouted moth beans (matki) cooked in a spicy gravy, topped with crunchy farsan, onion, lemon, and coriander, served with pav bread. Regional varieties: Pune Misal, Kolhapuri Misal (spiciest), Nashik Misal, and Mumbai Misal.

Maharashtra Arts

Lavani

Lavani is Maharashtra's most energetic folk dance-drama — a centuries-old tradition combining powerful rhythm, evocative poetry, and expressive performance. Traditionally performed by women in nine-yard sarees (nauvari) to the dholki drum. Songs are romantic, satirical, or socially critical. Two main styles: Nirguni (devotional) and Shringari (romantic). Patronised by the Maratha kings and Peshwas. Lavani is UNESCO-recognised and has significantly influenced Bollywood dance choreography.

Warli Painting

Warli painting is one of India's oldest art forms, created by the Warli tribe of North Maharashtra. Uses white pigment (traditionally rice paste) on a dark ochre or mud background, composed of circles, triangles, and squares. Human figures are depicted through two triangles joined at their tips. Scenes show harvests, weddings, hunting, dancing, and village life. Now recognised internationally — displayed in galleries across Europe and used in fashion and architecture.

Powada — Ballads of Maratha Valour

Powada are epic ballads — oral histories set to music celebrating the deeds of Shivaji Maharaj and Maratha warriors. Composed and performed by Shahirs (poet-musicians), accompanied by dholki, tutari (brass horn), and tasha (drum). The most famous Powadas were written by Shahir Agnidas and Shahir Tulsidas. Modern Shahirs compose Powadas about contemporary social issues. Powada performances are a highlight of Marathi cultural events in Europe.

Kirtan

Kirtan is the devotional performance tradition of the Varkari saints — a unique combination of music, philosophy, storytelling, and audience participation that has shaped Maharashtrian culture for 700 years. A Kirtankar narrates stories from saints' lives interspersed with abhangas of Dnyaneshwar, Tukaram, and Namdev. A performance typically lasts 2–4 hours. The Varkari movement (12th–17th centuries) produced Maharashtra's greatest spiritual poets whose abhangas are sung daily by millions.

Marathi Language

Language History

Marathi is one of the world's oldest living languages with a literary tradition spanning over 2,500 years. It belongs to the Indo-Aryan branch of the Indo-European language family, descended from Maharashtri Prakrit. Related to Hindi, Gujarati, and Bengali, but with a distinct grammar and vocabulary. Literary milestones: Dnyaneshwari (1290 CE) by Sant Dnyaneshwar; Dasbodh by Samarth Ramdas; abhangas of Sant Tukaram; modern writers P.L. Deshpande (Pu La) and Vijay Tendulkar.

Devanagari Script

Marathi is written in Devanagari — "script of the city of gods." Also used for Hindi, Sanskrit, and Nepali. Written left to right with a horizontal line (matra) connecting letters at the top. Marathi uses 48 characters (16 vowels and 36 consonants). Learning Devanagari takes most adults 1–2 weeks. Marathi cultural organisations across Europe run language and script classes for diaspora children.

Major Dialects

Marathi has over 40 distinct dialects. Standard Marathi (Pune dialect) is used in media and education. Varhadi is spoken in Vidarbha (Nagpur region) with a softer sound. Kolhapuri is the dialect of southern Maharashtra, known for its strength. Malvani or Konkani Marathi is spoken on the Konkan coast, influenced by Goan Portuguese. Ahirani is spoken in the Khandesh region. Thanjavuri Marathi is spoken by Marathi communities in Tamil Nadu, descendants of the Maratha rulers of Thanjavur.

Common Marathi Phrases

  • नमस्कार (Namaskar) — Hello / Greetings
  • कसे आहात? (Kase aahat?) — How are you? (formal)
  • धन्यवाद (Dhanyawad) — Thank you
  • कृपया (Krupaya) — Please
  • माफ करा (Maaf kara) — I'm sorry / Excuse me
  • मला मराठी येते (Mala Marathi yete) — I know Marathi
  • जय महाराष्ट्र! (Jai Maharashtra!) — Long live Maharashtra!
